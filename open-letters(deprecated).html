An Open Letter on the Meaning of Work

Remembering what we've forgotten about human activity

We've forgotten that work was never meant to be separate from life itself.

Industrial capitalism has reduced everything to "Work" (capital W) - jobs, careers, productivity metrics, economic output. This is work as commodity: something you do from 9-5 to earn money, separate from who you are and what you care about. It's about efficiency, competition, and extracting value from people and planet.

But "work" (lowercase w) - the way humans naturally contribute to life - has been almost completely lost. This is work as llankay in Quechua - one of three fundamental principles (alongside munay/love and yachay/wisdom) that can't be separated from each other. Llankay is work as tending: caring for children, growing food, healing relationships, creating beauty, solving problems together, maintaining community. It's work done with reverence for Pachamama (Mother Earth) and in service to the ayllu (extended community).

"In nature's economy the currency is not money, it is life."

- Vandana Shiva

In Andean cosmovision, all work is ayni - reciprocity that maintains cosmic balance. You work with the earth, ancestors, and community, not just manipulating resources. Work follows natural rhythms, serves the whole, and nourishes the worker as much as it produces results.

Buddhist understanding of right livelihood emphasizes work that supports rather than harms the web of life - one of the Noble Eightfold Path's essential practices. Hindu concepts like dharma frame work not as external obligation but as alignment with one's essential nature and role in the cosmic order. In many African languages, work and play share the same root - both understood as participation in the life-giving activities of community.

The Three Meanings of "Present"

When we speak of the "present of work," we invoke three profound dimensions that western industrial thinking has systematically separated:

Present as Presence: Full engagement with what is actually here, bringing our complete attention and authentic self to whatever we're tending. This is work done with mindfulness, with reverence for the living systems we're part of.

Present as Present Moment: Work rooted in this moment rather than anxious projection into future outcomes or dwelling in past failures. This is the Buddhist understanding of right action arising from clear seeing of what's needed now.

Present as Gift: Work as offering that strengthens rather than depletes the web of life. This is work as relational tending - both the giving and receiving that deepens our connections with each other, with community, with the more-than-human world.

A Gift is Not a Gift Until It Moves

In gift economies, the act of giving creates and maintains relationships rather than ending them. The gift keeps moving, weaving stronger bonds throughout the web of life. When we offer our gifts - our skills, attention, care, creativity - we're not depleting ourselves but participating in the circulation of life force that nourishes all.

These perspectives reveal what we've lost in the industrialization of human activity: work as presence (full engagement with what is), work as present (rooted in this moment rather than anxious projection), and work as present (offering that strengthens rather than depletes the commons).

With deep appreciation for the work we're all part of,
The Present of Work Network

To Leaders Seeking Transformation

On meeting situations rather than matching solutions

Dear Fellow Leaders,

You've probably invested significant resources in transformation initiatives. Maybe you've hired consultants, implemented change management frameworks, sent teams to leadership retreats. And perhaps, like many of us, you've discovered that most change efforts fail not because of poor execution, but because they treat organizations like machines to be fixed rather than living systems to be tended.

"The success of an intervention depends on the interior condition of the intervenor."

- Otto Scharmer

We want to share a crucial distinction that changed how we approach organizational transformation: meeting versus matching.

Matching vs Meeting

Matching means arriving with a solution and trying to fit the situation to your framework. Like a consultant who always recommends the same strategic planning process regardless of organizational culture, or implementing standard conflict resolution protocols without understanding the history of relationships.

Meeting means deep listening and curiosity about how a situation came to be, what's actually alive in it, and what wants to emerge. It's facilitating an Open Space session where the agenda emerges from participants' genuine passions, or spending time understanding the cultural context before designing any intervention.

Most transformation efforts fail because they try to match predetermined solutions to living situations rather than meeting what's actually present and responding to what wants to emerge.

Our Learning from Failure

This understanding comes from hard-won experience. Years ago, one of us led a change initiative inside a large corporation, building an incredible team of 25-30 volunteers and creating genuine momentum across departments. We were methodical, strategic, scientific - staying up nights sketching strategies and dreaming of transformation.

Yet despite our careful preparation, we missed something crucial: the organization's immune system. Our success threatened existing power structures, so we were gradually co-opted and eventually pushed out. The community we'd built was hurt, trust was damaged, relationships suffered. None of that was how we intended those dominoes to fall.

What we learned was that we had focused intensely on the mechanics of change while neglecting the relational field that could hold emergence. We had brilliant strategies but hadn't cultivated depth of relationship - both with individuals and with the living system of the organization itself.

A Different Approach

We now offer organizational support through methodologies that honor both efficiency and human flourishing:

  • Open Space Technology: Strategic planning that honors emergent rather than imposed direction
  • Art of Hosting: Leadership development for holding space across difference
  • Circle processes: Decision-making that serves the whole organization
  • Work Tech/Power Design: Human-AI relations that preserve culture during technology adoption

Our approach is capacity-building rather than dependency-creating. We facilitate while teaching your team to use these approaches themselves, addressing both relational and material dimensions of organizational change.

What This Looks Like in Practice

We helped an organization navigate major transition using council processes for decision-making, Open Space for visioning, and redesigning compensation structures to align with stated values, while teaching their leadership team to facilitate these approaches themselves. The result: sustainable change that continued long after our engagement ended.

If your organization is ready to try approaches that honor both productivity and human spirit, that build capacity rather than create dependency, we'd welcome a conversation about what might be possible.

In service to transformation that serves life,
The Present of Work Team

To Those Feeling Trapped in Conventional Work

You're not broken - the system is

Dear Fellow Traveler,

If you're reading this, you might be sitting at a desk wondering how you ended up here. Your daily activities feel disconnected from your deepest values and what you know needs healing in the world. You're good at your job, maybe even successful by conventional measures, but something essential is missing.

We want you to know: You're not broken. The system is.

You're experiencing what millions of people feel but rarely name: the profound disconnection between how we're organized to spend our days and what our souls actually long to contribute. You're not unmotivated or ungrateful - you're responding naturally to extractive systems that treat human activity as mere input for economic production.

"In the grammar of animacy, to be human is to be embedded in relationship, to live only in verb, as a service to the well-being of the whole."

- Robin Wall Kimmerer

What If Work Could Be Different?

What if your Monday mornings could feel like possibility instead of punishment? What if your daily activities could contribute to solutions rather than perpetuating problems? What if work could be a practice of presence, a gift to community, a participation in the ongoing creation of the world?

This isn't naive idealism. It's remembering what work has always been in cultures that understand humans as part of the web of life rather than separate from it.

Small Steps Toward Something Different

Transformation doesn't require quitting your job tomorrow and joining a commune. It starts with small experiments in bringing your whole self to whatever situation you're in:

  • Practice presence: Bring mindful attention to your current work, however mundane
  • Find your people: Connect with others who share your questions about meaningful work
  • Experiment with gift: Look for ways to offer your authentic gifts, even in small ways
  • Learn new approaches: Develop skills in facilitation, conflict navigation, or systems thinking
  • Support alternatives: Use your current resources to help build the more beautiful world your heart knows is possible

You're Part of a Larger Movement

You're not alone in this longing. Around the world, people are quietly creating new forms of work that serve life rather than extract from it. Worker cooperatives, gift economy experiments, regenerative businesses, commons-based organizing - all seeds of the future that's trying to emerge.

Your discontent isn't a personal failing. It's your soul's wisdom recognizing that humans were never meant to be cogs in machines. You're part of a species waking up to what becomes possible when work serves the flourishing of life.

Ready to Explore?

Join our monthly CommDojo to practice authentic communication with others asking similar questions. Attend our free community calls to explore what becomes possible when work becomes a practice of presence. You don't have to figure this out alone.

With deep respect for your journey,
The Present of Work Community

To Fellow Practitioners

On building the bridge while walking across it

Dear Colleagues in the Great Work,

You know the challenge we all face: how to create sustainable livelihood while serving transformation that the world desperately needs. How to integrate spiritual practice with practical effectiveness. How to avoid the trap of either selling out to systems that don't serve life or burning out trying to create change without adequate resources.

We write to you as fellow practitioners who have experienced both the successes and failures of trying to embody alternatives to extractive approaches in our own work and organizations.

What We've Learned Through Failure

Our previous iteration (TPOW 1.0) worked with tech companies, nonprofits, and the government of New Zealand, bringing what we believed was a more relational, emergence-based approach to transformation. But we discovered we had missed the material wisdom of how to hold partnerships while unconsciously slipping back into old industrial traps of time, profit, and efficiency.

Despite our evolved understanding of emergence, we hadn't solved the fundamental challenge of creating livelihood contexts where people could feel security and be truly held in relationship. People couldn't feel held in our relations. The absence of sustainable income and appropriate remuneration led to a critical loss of talent and vision.

This taught us perhaps the most important lesson yet: transformation work must be grounded in right relationship with material reality, not just with ideas and intentions. You cannot separate the spiritual, intellectual, and relational dimensions of work from the economic dimension without undermining the whole.

Our Current Approach

We've learned to organize through gift economy principles while maintaining practical sustainability through a hybrid model that separates educational work (fiscally sponsored through Inquiring Systems Institute) from consulting services (through our CrowdWork cooperative).

This structure allows us to maintain both accessibility and sustainability while staying true to our values:

  • Dhana (gift economy): Following Buddhist dana tradition where giving creates spiritual merit and community connection
  • Needs-based scholarships: No one turned away for lack of funds
  • Work-trade options: Contributing skills in exchange for participation
  • Standard pricing: Those with capacity support others' participation

Methodologies That Actually Work

Rather than inventing new techniques, we've found power in applying time-tested protocols that create conditions for Work (jobs) to align with work (sacred practice):

  • Open Space Technology: Meetings where passion and responsibility guide what emerges
  • Art of Hosting: Practices that allow collective intelligence to surface
  • Circle processes and council methods: Indigenous-rooted practices for decision-making
  • Warm data labs: Nora Bateson's methodology for exploring complex relationships
  • Transcontextual inquiry: Recognizing patterns across different domains

The Network as Learning Laboratory

Our three-layer ecosystem (Soil/Commons, Knots, Social Web) functions like a healthy forest where resources flow naturally between levels, creating abundance rather than scarcity. Each layer draws from and contributes back to the commons, following Elinor Ostrom's principles for managing shared resources.

An Invitation to Collaborate

We're not writing as experts who have figured it all out, but as fellow travelers sharing what we've learned through both triumph and failure. Our invitation is not to copy what we've done but to sense what wants to emerge in your own context, using principles that honor the gift dimension of human activity.

If you're developing skills in facilitation, organizational emergence, conflict navigation, or gift economy principles, we'd welcome collaboration. If you're wrestling with how to create sustainable livelihood while serving transformation, let's learn together.

In solidarity with the work we're all part of,
The Present of Work Practitioner Community

To Philanthropists & Funders

Exploring alternatives to extractive methods in resource flow

Dear Funders and Resource Stewards,

You've likely experienced the limitations of traditional philanthropic models - the way power dynamics persist even in "trust-based" approaches, how impact metrics can reduce complex social change to simplistic measures, how the very structure of having "funders" and "recipients" reinforces rather than transforms extractive relationships.

What if there were alternatives to extractive methods in how resources flow? What if wealth could serve the transformation of wealth relationships themselves?

Beyond Traditional Models

Our current focus through ATEM (Alternatives to Extractive Methods) is bringing together stakeholders from the philanthropy world - funders, recipients, intermediaries, and critics - to explore gift economy approaches beyond the usual frameworks of trust-based philanthropy, impact philanthropy, or effective altruism.

We're not interested in tweaking existing models. We're exploring what becomes possible when we approach philanthropy as gift economy, as relational tending, as participation in the web of life rather than management of charitable giving.

"A gift is not a gift until it moves. When we give it away, we ourselves come to receive the mysterious energy of the gift. A gift must always move."

- Lewis Hyde

What We're Learning

In healthy gift economies, giving creates and maintains relationships rather than ending them. The gift keeps moving, weaving stronger bonds throughout the web of life. This suggests fundamentally different approaches to resource flow:

  • Relationship-first funding: Long-term partnerships based on shared values rather than project-specific grants
  • Reciprocal learning: Funders as learning partners who are transformed by the work they support
  • Commons building: Investments that create shared resources rather than organizational dependencies
  • Cultural transformation: Supporting shifts in how wealth itself is understood and stewarded

Our Experiment

Through our fiscal sponsorship with Inquiring Systems Institute, we're experimenting with these principles ourselves. Educational work is supported through gift economy principles, while consulting work flows through our cooperative structure. This allows us to demonstrate alternatives while maintaining practical sustainability.

We're learning how to balance accessibility (no one turned away for lack of funds) with sustainability (paying facilitators fairly). How to design resource flows that deepen relationships rather than create dependency. How to measure success through qualitative indicators of relationship health rather than just quantitative outputs.

The Reimagining Philanthropy Lab

Our current ATEM lab is bringing together 12-15 people from across the philanthropy ecosystem for a 6-month exploration using warm data methodology. We're not seeking funding for predetermined programs, but creating space for new approaches to emerge from collective wisdom.

An Invitation

If you're interested in exploring alternatives to extractive methods in your own resource stewardship, we'd welcome conversation. This isn't about funding our work - it's about learning together how wealth can serve the transformation of wealth relationships themselves.

Perhaps you'd be interested in participating in our Reimagining Philanthropy lab, or supporting others' participation, or simply staying connected as we share what we're learning. The invitation is to relationship first, with resource flow emerging from authentic partnership rather than driving it.

With appreciation for your stewardship,
The Present of Work Team

Our Learning Journey

From corporate innovation to gift economy - lessons from failure

Dear Fellow Learners,

We want to share our story not as a success narrative, but as a learning journey marked by both breakthrough insights and painful failures. If you're working on transformation in any form, perhaps our experience offers useful lessons about what works, what doesn't, and why the journey matters more than the destination.

The Corporate Innovation Experiment

Years ago, one of us led a change initiative inside a large corporation, building an incredible team of 25-30 volunteers and creating genuine momentum across departments. We were methodical, strategic, scientific - so consumed with planning that nights were spent sketching strategies and dreaming of transformation.

Despite careful preparation, we missed something crucial: the organization's immune system. Our success threatened existing power structures, leading to gradual co-optation and eventual exile. The community we'd built was hurt, trust was damaged, relationships suffered.

The lesson: We had focused intensely on the mechanics of change while neglecting the relational field that could hold emergence. Brilliant strategies mean nothing without cultivating depth of relationship with both individuals and the living system itself.

SolvrX: The Consulting Trap

After being unable to continue as intrapreneurs, we started SolvrX as innovation consultants, working with companies like Verizon. Even with cutting-edge approaches and strong branding, we realized we had gotten away from what we truly cared about and couldn't stand out in a crowded marketplace.

We were still generating activity instead of flowing with what was already moving. Our approach remained mismatched to the environments we were operating in.

The lesson: Success metrics that ignore relationship and meaning will eventually lead to meaningless success. Technical competence without soul leads to spiritual emptiness.

CrowdWork: Learning Through Cooperation

SolvrX's failure taught us about the centrality of relationship in all change work. This led us to start CrowdWork as a worker cooperative, which continues operating today. We finally grasped that effective change means meeting situations deeply rather than trying to match them with predetermined solutions.

The lesson: Organizational form matters. Cooperative structures can embody values that hierarchical structures make nearly impossible to maintain.

TPOW 1.0: The Material Wisdom We Missed

Using lessons from CrowdWork, we launched The Present of Work (TPOW 1.0) as a consultancy, working with tech companies, nonprofits, the government of New Zealand, and coaching entrepreneurs. We thought we had integrated our past lessons into a more relational, emergence-based approach.

But we discovered we had missed the material wisdom of how to hold partnerships while unconsciously slipping back into old industrial traps of time, profit, and efficiency. Despite our evolved understanding of emergence, we hadn't solved the fundamental challenge of creating livelihood contexts where people could feel security and be truly held in relationship.

The absence of sustainable income and appropriate remuneration led to a critical loss of talent and vision. Our partnership dissolved because we had addressed philosophical dimensions while failing to tend the material substrate - the economic relationships that allow people to show up fully.

The lesson: You cannot separate the spiritual, intellectual, and relational dimensions of work from the economic dimension without undermining the whole. Transformation work must be grounded in right relationship with material reality, not just ideas and intentions.

TPOW 2.0: Gift Economy and Institutional Support

This recognition led us to our current iteration - a hybrid model that separates educational work (fiscally sponsored through Inquiring Systems Institute) from consulting services (through CrowdWork cooperative). We've learned to operate on gift economy principles while maintaining practical sustainability.

We're practicing what we call "meeting not matching" - deep acknowledgment and curiosity for how situations came to be, responding to what's actually present rather than imposing predetermined solutions. We understand ourselves as part of larger networks (learning, action, kinship, research, movement) rather than trying to build everything from scratch.

Current Structure

Educational work includes monthly dojos, ATEM labs, community resources - all operating through gift economy principles with extensive scholarship support. Consulting work maintains business flexibility while contributing to commons development. This allows us to demonstrate alternatives while ensuring sustainability.

What We're Still Learning

We're not perfect, and we want to be honest about ongoing limitations:

  • Economic realism gap: Our approaches work well for people with financial flexibility, but we haven't adequately addressed needs of people with significant financial obligations
  • Cultural bridge building: Our ecosystem currently attracts people already aligned with regenerative values. We're missing pathways for engaging people from different backgrounds
  • Scaling without losing soul: How do we maintain depth and quality as our network grows? How do we prevent commons dilution or appropriation?

The Pattern That Connects

Looking back, we see a pattern emerging through all our experiments: transformation happens through relationship, and relationship requires material conditions that allow people to show up fully. This includes economic relationships, power relationships, and our relationship with the material conditions that support life and work.

The future of work isn't something we need to engineer - it's something that wants to emerge through us when we show up with presence, curiosity, and reverence for the living web of relationships we're part of, including the economic relationships that support our capacity to do this work over time.

With gratitude for the journey and all who walk with us,
The Present of Work Team
