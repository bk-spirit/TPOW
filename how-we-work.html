How We Work

Our three-layer learning ecosystem, methodologies, and governance approaches

Our Learning Ecosystem: A Commons-Based Forest

We organize learning through three interconnected layers that function like a healthy forest ecosystem. Drawing from Elinor Ostrom's work on common resource pooling, each layer follows regenerative design principles where resources flow naturally between levels, creating abundance rather than scarcity.

Soil/Commons Layer - The Foundational Nutrients

Like rich forest soil that provides nutrients for all plant life, this layer contains our shared resources, wisdom, and relationships. Monthly community calls, open-source toolkits, our peer-to-peer podcast, and ongoing conversations create the fertile ground where everything else grows. Resources here are freely accessible and collectively maintained, following gift economy principles where contribution strengthens rather than depletes the commons.

Knots Layer - Where Individual Learning Happens

As Raimón Panikkar said, "A person is a knot in a net of relationships." Like specific trees and plants in a forest that create distinct growing environments, these are our dojos and guilds where people develop particular skills while tending the relationships that constitute them. CommDojo, Conflict Kinship Dojo, and learning guilds each create their own learning "microclimate" while drawing from and contributing back to the commons.

Social Web Layer - Where Collective Impact Happens

Like the forest canopy where different species interact and create conditions for rain and air circulation, this is where individual learning translates into collective action. Deep dive labs, organizational consulting, and cross-pollination between different learning communities create the conditions for larger systems transformation while feeding insights back down to enrich the commons.

How Resource Pooling Works:

Following Ostrom's principles, our commons are self-governed by participants rather than external authorities. People contribute what they can (time, skills, money, attention) and take what they need (learning, support, resources, connection). Clear agreements about participation and accountability prevent the "tragedy of the commons" while gift economy principles ensure abundance flows to everyone.

Our Core Methodologies

We use proven approaches that honor emergence over engineering and work with living systems rather than trying to control them:

Open Space Technology

Large group meetings where the agenda emerges from participants' genuine passions and responsibilities. Based on four principles: "Whoever comes is the right people," "Whatever happens is the only thing that could have," "Whenever it starts is the right time," and "When it's over, it's over." Perfect for organizational visioning and community gatherings where you need to honor diverse voices.

Art of Hosting

A collection of practices including circle processes, World Café, and appreciative inquiry that create conditions for collective intelligence to emerge. Focuses on hosting conversations that matter, with practices for holding space across difference and conflict. Includes training for leaders who want to facilitate emergence rather than control outcomes.

Circle Processes and Council Methods

Indigenous-rooted practices for decision-making and deep listening. Circle processes involve sitting in a circle where each person has equal voice and opportunity to speak and listen. Council methods create sacred space for wisdom to emerge from the collective, often using a talking piece that moves around the circle. These practices honor all voices and create consensus that serves the whole community.

Sociocracy

A governance system based on consent decision-making rather than consensus or hierarchy. Decisions are made when there are no remaining paramount objections (consent) rather than requiring everyone to agree (consensus). Uses a circle structure where each circle has a specific domain of authority and sends representatives to higher circles, creating both autonomy and connection across organizational levels.

Warm Data Labs

Developed by Nora Bateson, these are processes for exploring complex topics through multiple ways of knowing. Warm data is information about the interrelationships that integrate a complex system - not just data points in isolation, but the relationships, patterns, and connections that give systems their life. Labs bring together diverse stakeholders to perceive interdependencies through structured conversations that honor different wisdom traditions.

Gift Economy Principles

Economic relationships where giving creates and maintains relationships rather than ending them. Based on principles like dhana (Buddhist gift-giving that creates spiritual merit), needs-based accessibility (no one turned away for lack of funds), work-trade options, and structures where those with capacity support others' participation. Resources flow based on relationship and reciprocity rather than pure market exchange.

How to Engage at Each Layer

Soil/Commons Layer - Start Here if You're Curious

Free access to foundational resources that feed everything else:

  • Monthly community calls facilitated with Art of Hosting practices
  • Peer-to-peer podcast featuring practitioners sharing real successes and failures
  • Open-source toolkit using Creative Commons licensing
  • Connection with practitioners across different fields

Knots Layer - Deepen Your Practice

Skill-building communities that honor both individual development and collective wisdom:

CommDojo (Communication Dojo)

Monthly practice using T-group and Bohm dialogue for authentic communication. T-group involves unstructured group interaction to learn about group dynamics and personal communication patterns. Bohm dialogue creates shared meaning through suspension of assumptions and deep listening.

Conflict Kinship Dojo

Monthly gatherings using restorative justice circles, nonviolent communication, and council processes. Explores conflict as creative force rather than something to avoid. Restorative justice focuses on healing and accountability rather than punishment.

Learning Guilds

8-12 week intensive programs including Organizational Emergence Guild (working with institutional immune systems) and Economic Livelihood Guild (exploring gift economy principles). Combines weekly sessions with mentorship and peer coaching.

Soul Space

Embodied fishbowl practice open to the public. Fishbowl involves a small group having a conversation in the center while others listen, with opportunities to join the inner circle when moved to contribute.

Social Web Layer - Collective Action and Deep Inquiry

Where individual learning translates into collective impact:

  • ATEM Labs: Alternatives to Extractive Methods explorations using warm data methodology. Current focus: Reimagining Philanthropy bringing together funders, recipients, and intermediaries.
  • Organizational Support: Consulting and facilitation through CrowdWork cooperative that builds capacity rather than creates dependency.
  • Transformative Services: Journey coaching, Work Tech/Power Design for human-AI relations, custom retreats and gatherings.

Access and Pricing Philosophy

We operate with multiple levels of resourcing based on gift economy principles:

Our Approach to Money and Access:

  • Dhana (Gift Economy): Contribute what feels right based on your capacity, following Buddhist dana tradition where giving creates spiritual merit and community connection
  • Needs-based Scholarships: No one turned away for lack of funds - transformation work must be accessible
  • Standard Pricing: For those with capacity to support their own participation plus others' scholarships
  • Work-trade: Contribute skills (facilitation, design, documentation) in exchange for participation
  • Organizational Sponsorship: Companies fund community member access as part of their own learning

Governance and Decision-Making

We use sociocratic principles adapted for network organizing:

How Decisions Get Made

  • Consent-based: Decisions proceed when there are no remaining paramount objections, not when everyone agrees
  • Circle structure: Different domains (community learning, deep dives, organizational support) have autonomy within their areas
  • Community input: Major direction changes discussed in monthly calls and documented openly
  • Conflict resolution: Using our own conflict navigation practices - circle processes and restorative approaches

Organizational Structure

Educational and research work is fiscally sponsored through Inquiring Systems Institute. Consulting and fee-for-service work happens through CrowdWork cooperative. This separation allows us to maintain gift economy principles for learning while ensuring legal protection and sustainability for professional services.

Our Learning Philosophy

Drawing from Nora Bateson's work on mutual learning, we understand learning as the continuous process of adjusting relationships within living systems rather than downloading information.

Learning Through Doing Together

Instead of traditional instruction, we use collaborative practice: co-hosting gatherings where you develop facilitation skills in real-time, participating in organizational transformation projects where you learn systems thinking by engaging with actual systems, joining research cohorts where you develop expertise through collaborative inquiry with diverse practitioners.

Transcontextual Learning

We recognize that patterns operating in one domain can inform organizing in another. Insights from mycorrhizal networks inform organizational resource sharing. Indigenous council processes transform contemporary decision-making. Sacred geometry appears in both mathematics and ceremonial practice. This isn't metaphorical thinking - it's understanding that the same principles of life, relationship, and emergence apply across all living systems.
