About Us

Origins, vision, principles, and the people behind the work

Our Origins

The Present of Work began six years ago as a practice community, co-originating with Starter Cultures—"a village of practice, a nurturing space to learn, grow, and heal together."

Founded by practitioners who experienced firsthand the disconnection between conventional work structures and human flourishing, we started as a small group exploring what regenerative work relationships might look like.

Like fermentation cultures that break down what was into something new and nourishing, our work creates conditions for the transformation of work itself. What began as intimate conversations about meaning and purpose has evolved into a living commons—a shared space where we're building platforms of relationships and weaving networks of meaning that enable people to live lives of purpose and contribution.

Our Structure

Rather than operating as a traditional organization, we function as a commons-based ecosystem. This means we're collectively stewarded rather than owned, focused on creating shared value rather than extracting profit, and designed to grow through contribution rather than consumption. Our various business applications and professional services exist to support and resource the commons, not the other way around.

What We're Working Toward

Our Vision

We envision communities of practice sprouting everywhere—"The Present of Education," "The Present of Healthcare," "The Present of Technology"—each applying these frameworks to their domain while contributing insights back to the broader ecosystem. We see new organizational forms emerging that operate from principles of wholeness, regeneration, and shared prosperity.

This isn't about replacing existing systems overnight, but creating parallel structures and demonstrating what becomes possible when we organize around our full humanity.

Why Now?

The old models of work clearly aren't serving us or the planet. Climate change, inequality, mental health crises, and social fragmentation are all connected to extractive approaches to human activity. At the same time, we have unprecedented opportunities to create new forms of collaboration, learning, and impact.

Core Principles

These aren't just nice ideas—they're our core beliefs that actively guide how we make decisions, design gatherings, approach projects, and relate to each other. They represent a fundamental shift from extractive to regenerative ways of being and working together.

Wholeness

We recognize that humans are whole beings, not fragmented resources. Our work honors the full spectrum of human experience rather than treating people as partial selves. For example, instead of checking emotions at the door, we might start meetings by acknowledging how people are actually feeling and what they need to show up fully.

Fractal Thinking

Patterns that serve transformation at the individual level also serve at team, organizational, and societal levels. The work we do on ourselves mirrors and supports the work we do in the world. Learning to listen deeply to our own inner conflicts helps us facilitate better dialogue in team disputes.

Systems Awareness

We approach challenges through a systems lens, recognizing that lasting change happens through shifting relationships and patterns rather than fixing isolated problems. This might mean addressing workplace stress by examining organizational culture and decision-making processes, not just offering individual wellness programs.

Complexity Consciousness

We embrace uncertainty, emergence, and non-linear change. Rather than trying to control outcomes, we create conditions for transformation and trust in organic intelligence. Instead of rigid strategic plans, we might use sensing practices to notice what wants to emerge and adapt accordingly.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why focus on "work" specifically?

Work is where most adults spend the majority of their waking hours, yet it's often the domain of life most disconnected from our values, creativity, and sense of purpose. As anthropologist David Graeber argued in "Bullshit Jobs," much of contemporary work feels meaningless even to those doing it. Political theorist Kathi Weeks challenges us in "The Problem with Work" to imagine life beyond the work ethic that subordinates all other values to productivity.

We see work as both a site of exploitation AND a potential space of liberation. When we transform our relationship with work, we transform our relationship with power, community, and meaning itself.

What influenced this approach?

Critical work theory: Kathi Weeks' critique of the work ethic, David Graeber's analysis of meaningless labor, and feminist economists' recognition of care work as essential but devalued.

Systems thinking: Nora Bateson's "warm data" approach to understanding complex relationships, her father Gregory Bateson's insights into patterns that connect, and ecological thinking that sees humans as part of larger living systems.

Decolonial perspectives: Bayo Akomolafe's work on "coming home to indigenous ways of knowing" and his challenge to Western frameworks of change and progress.

Contemplative traditions: Buddhist concepts of right livelihood, Stoic practices of aligning action with values, and Indigenous approaches to reciprocity with the living world.

Alternative economics: Gift economy research, cooperative movement history, and contemporary experiments in post-capitalist organizing.

What's the history and origins of this work?

Six years ago, a small group of practitioners were experiencing firsthand the disconnection between conventional work structures and human flourishing. Many had "successful" careers but felt spiritually depleted, relationally isolated, or complicit in systems causing harm.

We began as intimate conversations about meaning and purpose, co-originating with Starter Cultures around the question: "How can we create conditions for human transformation that serve both individual flourishing and collective change?" Like fermentation cultures that transform raw ingredients into nourishment, we began creating conditions for the transformation of work itself.

What started as personal questioning evolved into collaborative research, then community practice, then frameworks others could adapt. We discovered we weren't alone—people everywhere were asking similar questions about how to align their daily activities with their deepest values.

How is this different from other "future of work" approaches?

Most "future of work" discourse focuses on adapting humans to technological change—remote work tools, gig economy platforms, AI collaboration. We're interested in the deeper question: How do we organize human activity to serve life rather than extract from it?

Rather than making work more efficient, we're asking whether current definitions of productivity actually serve human and planetary wellbeing. Instead of optimizing individuals for existing systems, we're experimenting with new systems that honor human wholeness.

What We're Still Learning

We're not perfect, and we want to be honest about our limitations and blind spots:

Economic Realism Gap

Our approaches work well for people with some financial flexibility, but we haven't adequately addressed the needs of people with significant financial obligations—mortgages, student loans, medical debt, family support responsibilities—who can't easily transition to potentially lower-income regenerative work. We're actively seeking input from people in these circumstances to design more realistic pathways.

Cultural Bridge Building

Our ecosystem currently attracts people already somewhat aligned with regenerative values. We recognize we're missing pathways for engaging people from conservative backgrounds, traditional business environments, or those skeptical of alternative economic models. We want to invite voices from these communities to help us understand how to build approaches that work for them.

Accountability and Course Correction

While we emphasize emergence and experimentation, we're still developing robust ways to measure success and address situations where regenerative experiments cause unintended harm or simply don't work as intended. We need better feedback loops and more honest assessment of when our approaches aren't serving people well.

How You Can Help

If you represent perspectives or experiences we're missing, we want to learn from you. Whether you're aligned with our current direction or represent a perspective we're missing, we want to hear from you. Our goal isn't to create an echo chamber of like-minded people, but to develop approaches to work and economics that can serve human flourishing across different backgrounds, circumstances, and value systems.

Email us: contact@presentofwork.org with your perspective on what we're missing, specific challenges you face that our current pathways don't address, or ideas for how we could better serve people from your background or circumstances.
